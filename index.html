Her English superscore: 16/36  ·  Target: 22  ·  75 questions, 45 min (extended time ~67 min)
Conventions of Standard English 24% on her test
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
Punctuation (commas, semicolons, colons, apostrophes), sentence structure (run-ons, fragments, parallel structure), subject-verb agreement, pronoun agreement, and verb tense consistency. These are the most mechanical, rule-based questions on the whole test — and the most learnable.
The most common traps (she likely missed these)
Comma splices: Two complete sentences joined with just a comma. The ACT loves making students pick between a comma, semicolon, or period. Rule: a semicolon can replace a period; a comma alone cannot join two full sentences.
Wrong: "She studied hard, she passed the test."
Right: "She studied hard; she passed the test." or "She studied hard, and she passed the test."
Apostrophes: Its vs. It's. Their vs. They're. Possessive pronouns never use apostrophes — only contractions do.
Subject-verb agreement with tricky middles: "The box of chocolates [is/are] on the table." The verb agrees with "box" (singular), not "chocolates."
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
Ask her to read the sentence aloud and stop at every punctuation mark. Does it feel like a full stop? Then it probably needs a period or semicolon, not a comma. For apostrophes, drill the single rule: possessive pronouns (its, their, your, whose) NEVER have apostrophes. Write them on a sticky note on her laptop.

Knowledge of Language 29% on her test
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
Word choice (precision, conciseness), style (avoiding redundancy), and transitions between ideas. The ACT loves testing whether you can cut unnecessary words — shorter is almost always correct when meaning is preserved.
The golden rule: shortest answer that preserves meaning
Wrong: "Due to the fact that she was tired, she went to bed early."
Right: "Because she was tired, she went to bed early."
Redundancy trap: Watch for answers that say the same thing twice. "End result," "past history," "future plans" — the ACT will offer these as wrong answers. The correct choice cuts the redundant word.
Transitions: "However," "Therefore," "Furthermore," "For example" — know what each one signals (contrast, conclusion, addition, example). The ACT asks you to pick the right transition word for the relationship between two sentences.
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
Play a game: give her a wordy sentence and ask her to cut it in half without losing meaning. This is actually a great Graphic Design parallel — you know that good design removes everything unnecessary. Same rule applies here. Less is more.

Production of Writing (Rhetorical Skills) 63% on her test — her strongest!
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
Organization, relevance, and purpose. Questions like "Should this sentence be added or deleted?", "Which sentence best introduces this paragraph?", "Does this sentence support the author's argument?" These are judgment calls about writing quality, not grammar rules.
Key strategies
Always read the question first — rhetorical questions always tell you what to look for. "Which sentence best supports the paragraph's main idea?" means you need to understand the paragraph's main idea before you can answer.
Deletion questions: If a sentence adds new, unrelated information, delete it. If it supports or clarifies the paragraph's point, keep it.
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
This is her strongest area — protect it. If she misses one, ask her to explain the paragraph's main idea in one sentence first, then re-read the question. Usually the answer becomes obvious once the main idea is clear.
Her Reading superscore: 20/36  ·  Target: 25  ·  4 passages, 40 questions, 35 min (extended ~52 min)  ·  Most inconsistent section — dropped from 20 to 15 between tests
Key Ideas and Details 50% on her best test
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
Main idea questions, detail questions ("According to the passage..."), and inference questions ("The author implies..."). This is the largest category — 12 of 40 questions.
The passage approach that works
Step 1 — Preview questions first: Skim ALL 10 questions before reading a word of the passage. Circle any that reference a specific line or detail.
Step 2 — Read actively: Underline the topic sentence of each paragraph. After each paragraph, ask "what was that about?" in one phrase.
Step 3 — Answer as you go: If a question references lines 12-15, go straight there — don't re-read the whole passage.
For "According to the passage..." questions — the answer is always directly stated somewhere. Never use outside knowledge. Find the line, point to the answer.
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
Her big drop (20→15) is almost certainly a focus issue, not a knowledge issue. Practice this: after each paragraph she reads, she must say aloud what it was about before moving on. This keeps her anchored and fights the ADHD drift. Her extended time makes this very doable.

Craft and Structure 50% on her best test
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
Author's purpose ("Why does the author include..."), word meaning in context ("As used in line 14, 'charged' most nearly means..."), and point of view. These require understanding WHY something was written, not just WHAT it says.
Vocabulary in context — the big trick
"As used in line 22, 'green' most nearly means..." — the answer is almost never the most common meaning of the word. Always go back to the line and read 2 sentences before and after to understand context.
Author's purpose questions: Ask "why would a writer include this?" Usually the answer is: to support an argument, to give a counterexample, to provide evidence, or to introduce a contrast.
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
For vocab-in-context: cover the answer choices, read the sentence with the word removed, and fill in your own word. Then find the answer that matches your word. This prevents her from being tricked by the obvious meaning.
Integration of Knowledge and Ideas 57% on her best test — good foundation!
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
Comparing multiple sources, evaluating evidence, identifying how an argument is structured. The paired-passage question (two related passages) falls here — students must compare authors' viewpoints.
Paired passage strategy
Read Passage A, answer Passage A questions. Read Passage B, answer Passage B questions. Then answer the comparison questions. Never try to hold both passages in memory at once.
Comparison questions: "Both authors would agree that..." — find something both passages actually say, not something you think both authors believe.
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
Have her physically write "A says: ___" and "B says: ___" before answering comparison questions. This forces her to anchor her answer in the text and not drift into assumptions.
Her Science superscore: 19/36  ·  Target: 27  ·  6 passages, 40 questions, 35 min (extended ~52 min)  ·  This is NOT a science knowledge test — it's reading comprehension with graphs
Interpretation of Data (graphs & tables) 50% on her test
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
Reading values from graphs, identifying trends (as X increases, Y...), comparing data between figures, and interpolating/extrapolating values. Most questions send you directly to a specific figure — this is a lookup task, not a thinking task.
The approach — figures first, always
Step 1: Scan figure titles and axis labels BEFORE reading the passage text. Know what each graph measures.
Step 2: Read the question, find which figure it references, go straight there.
Step 3: Trace the value with your finger (or pencil on paper). Don't estimate — be precise.
Trend questions: "As temperature increases, the reaction rate..." — look at whether the line goes up, down, or stays flat. That IS the answer.
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
Practice graph reading with ANY graph — news articles, weather charts, anything. Ask "what's the x-axis? What's the y-axis? What happens to Y when X doubles?" This builds the reflex of reading axes before anything else.

Scientific Investigation (experiments) 25% on her test — biggest Science gap
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
Understanding experimental design — what is being tested (independent variable), what is being measured (dependent variable), what stays the same (control), and what would make the experiment better. This is her lowest Science subscore and a huge opportunity.
The three things every experiment has
Independent variable: What the scientist changes on purpose. "In Experiment 1, the temperature was varied from 20°C to 80°C" — temperature is independent.
Dependent variable: What gets measured as a result. Usually what's on the Y-axis of the graph.
Control: What stays the same to make the test fair. "All other conditions were held constant."
Question: "What was the independent variable in Experiment 2?" → Look for what the scientists deliberately changed. It's always stated in the setup paragraph.
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
Use the "CSI method" — have her label every experiment: C (control), I (independent variable), D (dependent variable) before answering any questions. Takes 20 seconds and eliminates most mistakes. Make it a habit she does automatically.
Evaluation of Models & Conflicting Viewpoints 40% on her test
▼
What the ACT actually tests here
The "Conflicting Viewpoints" passage is unique — it's the only passage that requires reading the text carefully (like Reading). Two or three scientists present different explanations for the same phenomenon. You have to understand what each scientist believes and how they differ.
The key difference from other Science passages
For all other Science passages: figures first, text second. For Conflicting Viewpoints: read the text fully, then answer questions. The figures (if any) are secondary.
Make a quick chart: "Scientist 1 believes ___ because ___. Scientist 2 believes ___ because ___." Questions will ask which viewpoint is supported by a new piece of evidence.
New evidence question: "A study finds that X increases with Y. This finding most supports..." → Go to your chart. Which scientist predicted that relationship?
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
Have her read a Conflicting Viewpoints passage and summarize each scientist's view in one sentence before touching the questions. If she can do that clearly, she'll get most of the questions right. Save these passages for last during the actual test — they take the most reading time.
🎓 The Feynman Technique in action: For each concept below, have her explain it to you like you're a student who's never seen it. If she gets stuck or the explanation doesn't make sense, you've both just found a gap that needs more tutor time. No pressure — it's actually fun, and it works.
Her Math superscore: 17/36  ·  Target: 22  ·  60 questions, 60 min (extended ~90 min)
Statistics & Probability 0% on BOTH tests — top priority
▼
What she needs to be able to explain to you
Mean, median, mode: Mean = average (add all, divide by count). Median = middle value when sorted. Mode = most frequent value. Ask her: "If I have scores of 70, 85, 90, 90, 100 — what's the mean? The median? The mode?"
Probability: Probability = favorable outcomes ÷ total outcomes. Ask her: "If I pull a card from a deck of 52, what's the probability of getting a heart?" (13/52 = 1/4)
Probability of two events: "AND" = multiply. "OR" = add (and subtract overlap). Ask her to explain this with a coin flip example.
You ask: "I roll a die twice. What's the probability of getting a 6 both times?" She should say: 1/6 × 1/6 = 1/36. If she can't — more tutor work needed here.
🔁 Signs she needs more tutor time on this
If she can't explain mean vs. median clearly, or confuses "AND" vs. "OR" probability — flag this for the tutor immediately. These 6 questions (0% right = 6 wrong) are the single biggest quick-win on the whole test.

Algebra & Expressions 25% on her test
▼
What she needs to be able to explain to you
Solving for x: Whatever you do to one side, do to the other. Ask her: "Solve 3x + 7 = 22." (x = 5)
Systems of equations: Two equations, two unknowns. She should be able to solve by substitution or elimination. Ask her to walk you through one step by step.
Factoring: x² + 5x + 6 = (x+2)(x+3). Ask her: "How do you know which numbers to use?" She should say: find two numbers that multiply to 6 and add to 5.
You ask: "If 2x - 3 = 11, what is x?" She should say: add 3 to both sides (2x = 14), then divide by 2 (x = 7). If she needs a calculator for this — more algebra drilling needed.
🔁 Signs she needs more tutor time on this
If she can't factor a simple quadratic or gets confused with negative numbers in equations — flag for tutor. Note: her SAT algebra score improved, so she may be stronger here than the ACT score shows.

Functions 29% on her test
▼
What she needs to be able to explain to you
Function notation: f(x) means "plug x into the rule." Ask her: "If f(x) = 2x + 3, what is f(4)?" (Answer: 11)
Reading graphs of functions: Where does the line cross the x-axis (zeros)? Where does it cross the y-axis (y-intercept)? Is it increasing or decreasing?
Domain and range: Domain = all valid x values (inputs). Range = all valid y values (outputs). Ask her to explain using a simple graph.
You ask: "Draw me a function. Now tell me its domain and range." She should point to the x-axis spread for domain, y-axis spread for range. If she's confused — flag for tutor.
🔁 Signs she needs more tutor time on this
If she can't evaluate f(x) for a given value or doesn't know what a y-intercept is — these are fundamental and need tutor attention before test day.

Geometry 86% on her test — her Math superpower!
▼
What the ACT tests here
Area and perimeter of shapes, angle relationships, the Pythagorean theorem, coordinate geometry (slope, distance, midpoint), and basic trig (SOH-CAH-TOA). She's already great at this — 86% is excellent.
How to use this as a confidence builder
When she's feeling discouraged about Stats or Algebra, open a geometry problem set and let her crush it. Remind her: she's already mastered almost 7/8 geometry questions. She has real math ability — she just needs to extend it to other areas.
Ask her: "What's the area of a triangle with base 8 and height 5?" (20) "What's the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs 3 and 4?" (5) She'll get these. Use it to boost her confidence before harder topics.
🔁 Protect this score
Don't neglect geometry entirely even though she's good at it. 5 minutes of geometry practice a week keeps the skill sharp and gives her an easy confidence boost heading into test day.
Modeling & Word Problems 18–21% on her tests
▼
What she needs to be able to explain to you
Setting up equations from word problems: This is the #1 skill. Ask her to read a word problem aloud and tell you what the variable represents before doing any math.
Rate problems: Distance = Rate × Time. If a car travels 60 mph for 2.5 hours, how far? (150 miles) Ask her to set it up, not just solve it.
Proportion and percent: "What is 35% of 80?" She should set up 0.35 × 80 = 28. "What percent of 50 is 12?" She should set up 12/50 × 100 = 24%.
You ask: "A shirt costs $40. It's on sale for 25% off. What's the sale price?" She should say: 40 × 0.25 = $10 off, so $30. If she reaches for a calculator before setting up the equation — that's the habit to fix.
🔁 If she keeps missing these — how to re-teach
The most common mistake is jumping to calculations before understanding what the question is asking. Teach her to write one sentence: "I am looking for ___." Then set up the equation. This slows the impulsive first-answer trap that ADHD brains fall into on word problems.
